# Composition — axes, geometry, and scale

Lay the **bones** of the garden first — the axes and the geometry — before any
bed, hedge, or tree. The structure is what makes a designed garden read as
designed.

## Axes

Most formal traditions are organized on an axis:

- **Bilateral** — one dominant central axis with mirror symmetry across it.
  The French formal default; the garden extends the building's centerline.
- **Cross-axial** — a primary axis crossed by one or more secondary axes; the
  Mughal char bagh crosses two water channels at the center.
- **Radial** — paths spoke out from a central feature; suits a circular plaza
  or a star-shaped bosquet.
- **Sequenced (no axis)** — a Japanese stroll garden has *no* axial geometry;
  it is composed as a series of framed viewpoints along a route. Lay the
  viewpoints and the path between them instead of an axis.

Establish the **primary axis** as a coordinate vector and origin; place every
element relative to it.

## Vistas

A long straight line must **end on something** — a fountain, a statue, a
gate, a building, a specimen tree. An axis that terminates on nothing is a
wasted one. Place the eye-catchers, then run the axes to them.

## Symmetry and geometry

- In a symmetric tradition, what is on one side of the axis is mirrored on the
  other — parterres, basins, allées, paths. Build one half, mirror it.
- Every curve is **justified**: a circle or ellipse arc, a regular polygon, or
  a tradition's specific sinuous line (an English-landscape lake edge). No
  freehand wobble — that is `terraforming`'s domain, not yours.
- Proportion matters more than size — a parterre's length-to-width ratio, a
  reflecting pool's 1:3 to 1:8 rectangle, the golden-ratio placement of a zen
  garden's stones.

## Zones

Grade the garden from the building outward, formal to looser:

- **Near the house** — the most ornate, controlled elements (parterres de
  broderie, knot gardens).
- **The middle** — allées, bosquets, lawns, the main water feature.
- **The far edge** — looser planting, a transition to the wild or the wall.

## Scale

- A small garden — a courtyard, a zen garden — builds at **1:1**.
- A Tier 2+ garden (a chateau's grounds, a royal park) builds at **1:4**, or
  1:8 for the very largest. Keep the *proportions* exact at the reduced scale;
  surface the ratio to the user.

## Tiling

A garden over 64 blocks in any axis is split into structure tiles. **Put every
seam on an axis of symmetry or along a path or hedge line** — never bisect a
parterre, a basin, or a fountain. A seam on the centerline is invisible; a
seam through a pattern shows.

## The transition edge

Where a geometric garden meets naturalistic terrain, do not let the grid end
in a hard line. Plan a **transition apron** — a band of mixed ground (gravel
fading to grass), looser planting, an irregular hedge edge — so the designed
garden resolves into the wild rather than colliding with it.
